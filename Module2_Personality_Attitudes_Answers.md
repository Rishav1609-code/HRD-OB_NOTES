## ❓ Question 1: Define extraversion and introversion. Discuss how an understanding of attitudes is useful for the study of OB. What is introvert and extrovert personality? What is trait theory of personality?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Extraversion** is a personality dimension characterized by sociability, assertiveness, talkativeness, and a preference for external stimulation. Extraverts draw energy from social interactions.

**Introversion** is the opposite — characterized by quietness, reflection, and preference for solitude. Introverts draw energy from within and prefer depth over breadth in social interactions.

**Attitude** is a learned predisposition to respond consistently (positively or negatively) toward a given object, person, or situation. Understanding attitudes helps predict and explain employee behaviour in organizations.

**Trait Theory of Personality** holds that personality consists of broad, stable traits that persist across situations and time. Traits are measurable dimensions of personality that differentiate individuals.

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Extraversion vs. Introversion**

> | Dimension       | Extravert                          | Introvert                            |
> |-----------------|-------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
> | Energy Source   | External world (people, activities) | Internal world (thoughts, ideas)     |
> | Communication   | Talks freely, thinks aloud          | Listens more, thinks before speaking |
> | Work Style      | Prefers teamwork, open offices      | Prefers independent work, quiet      |
> | Leadership      | Natural leaders, visible            | Thoughtful leaders, behind the scenes|
> | Motivation      | Recognition, social rewards         | Autonomy, mastery                    |
>
> Both types are equally capable — organizations benefit from **balancing both** in teams.

**Key Point 2: How Understanding Attitudes is Useful in OB**

> Attitudes influence **three levels of behaviour:**
>
> - **Cognitive Component** → What a person *believes* (e.g., "My job is meaningful")
> - **Affective Component** → What a person *feels* (e.g., "I love my work")
> - **Behavioural Component** → What a person *does* (e.g., works extra hours)
>
> Usefulness in OB:
> 1. **Predicting Behaviour** — Positive attitudes lead to higher performance
> 2. **Job Satisfaction** — Attitudes directly affect how satisfied employees feel
> 3. **Organizational Commitment** — Positive attitudes increase loyalty
> 4. **Change Management** — Understanding attitudes helps manage resistance
> 5. **Conflict Resolution** — Attitude differences often cause workplace conflicts

**Key Point 3: Trait Theory of Personality**

> Proposed by **Gordon Allport**, **Raymond Cattell**, and later expanded by **Costa & McCrae**:
>
> - **Cardinal Traits** — Dominant trait that shapes all behaviour (e.g., Gandhi's non-violence)
> - **Central Traits** — Key characteristics that describe a person (honest, kind, shy)
> - **Secondary Traits** — Situational, less consistent traits (preferences, habits)
>
> Cattell identified **16 personality factors (16PF)** through factor analysis.
> The modern extension is the **Big Five Model (OCEAN)** — the most widely accepted trait theory.
>
> Trait theory is useful in OB because:
> - Helps predict job performance
> - Guides employee selection and placement
> - Identifies leadership potential

## ❓ Question 2: Explain the characteristics and components of attitude. Define the relationship of personality with OB. Define personality. Describe the major determinants of personality.

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Attitude** is a persistent mental and emotional readiness to react to persons, objects, or situations in a particular way. It is shaped by experience, culture, and socialization.

**Personality** is the sum total of ways in which an individual reacts to and interacts with others. It is the unique and relatively stable pattern of thoughts, emotions, and behaviours that characterize a person.

> 📌 *"Personality is the dynamic organisation within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine his unique adjustment to the environment."* — Gordon Allport

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Characteristics of Attitude**

> 1. **Learned** — Attitudes are not innate; they are formed through experience and observation
> 2. **Persistent** — Once formed, attitudes tend to be stable and long-lasting
> 3. **Directional** — They have a positive or negative direction (favouring or opposing)
> 4. **Valenced** — They carry intensity (strong vs. weak attitude)
> 5. **Consistent** — Attitudes are consistent with related behaviours (though not perfectly)
> 6. **Object-Specific** — Every attitude is directed at a specific attitude object
> 7. **Social** — Attitudes are formed and changed through social interactions

**Key Point 2: Components of Attitude (ABC Model)**

> - **A — Affective Component (Feeling):**
>   What you *feel* about the attitude object.
>   Example: "I feel happy at my job."
>
> - **B — Behavioural Component (Action):**
>   How you *intend to act* or actually act.
>   Example: "I always arrive early to work."
>
> - **C — Cognitive Component (Belief):**
>   What you *think or believe* about the attitude object.
>   Example: "I believe my company values its employees."
>
> All three components are interrelated — a change in one often triggers changes in the others.

**Key Point 3: Determinants of Personality**

> Personality is shaped by multiple factors:
>
> 1. **Heredity (Biological Factors):**
>    Genetics, physical structure, brain chemistry influence personality traits. Twin studies show 50% of personality variance is inherited.
>
> 2. **Environment (Situational Factors):**
>    Culture, family upbringing, friends, religion, education, and life experiences shape personality significantly.
>
> 3. **Situation:**
>    Different situations bring out different aspects of personality. A person may behave differently in office vs. at home.
>
> 4. **Family and Social Background:**
>    Parent-child relationships, birth order, and family values are powerful determinants.
>
> 5. **Culture:**
>    National and regional culture instills values and behavioural norms that shape personality.

**Key Point 4: Relationship of Personality with OB**

> - **Job Performance** — Conscientiousness (a personality trait) is the best predictor of job performance
> - **Leadership** — Extraversion and openness predict leadership emergence
> - **Team Effectiveness** — Agreeableness predicts cooperation and team harmony
> - **Job Fit** — Personality-job match (Holland's theory) leads to higher satisfaction and lower turnover
> - **Stress Response** — Neuroticism predicts emotional instability under pressure

## ❓ Question 3: Show the relationship of job satisfaction with employee performance, employee turnover and absenteeism. What are the different ways of employees' expression of their dissatisfaction?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Job Satisfaction** refers to the positive emotional state resulting from appraisal of one's job or job experiences. It is how employees feel about their jobs — whether their needs and expectations are being met.

When employees are dissatisfied, it manifests in predictable patterns that affect organizational performance through changes in productivity, absenteeism, and turnover.

> 📌 *"Job satisfaction is a pleasurable or positive emotional state resulting from the appraisal of one's job or job experiences."* — Locke (1976)

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Job Satisfaction & Employee Performance**

> - **Moderate Positive Correlation:** Research shows satisfied employees tend to be more productive, but the relationship is not perfect (r ≈ 0.30).
> - **Satisfied workers** show:
>   - Higher quality output
>   - More creativity and innovation
>   - Better customer service
>   - Higher Organizational Citizenship Behaviour (OCB)
> - **The Spillover Effect:** Job satisfaction spills over to overall life satisfaction, which in turn reinforces better performance.
> - **Performance → Satisfaction:** High performance that is *rewarded* leads to satisfaction (reverse causality also exists).

**Key Point 2: Job Satisfaction & Employee Turnover**

> - **Strong Negative Correlation:** Higher job satisfaction → Lower turnover.
> - Dissatisfied employees:
>   - Actively search for alternative employment
>   - Are easily poached by competitors
>   - Leave once a better opportunity arises
> - **Turnover is costly** — recruitment, training, and knowledge loss cost 50–200% of annual salary per departed employee.
> - **Moderating factors:** Job market conditions, skills scarcity, organizational loyalty can moderate the satisfaction-turnover link.

**Key Point 3: Job Satisfaction & Absenteeism**

> - **Moderate Negative Correlation:** Dissatisfied employees miss work more frequently.
> - Types of absenteeism caused by dissatisfaction:
>   - **Involuntary** — illness (stress-induced)
>   - **Voluntary** — mental health days, avoiding toxic workplace
> - **Stress & Burnout** from dissatisfaction lead to physical and psychological health issues.
> - High absenteeism = lost productivity, cost to replace coverage, team morale damage.

**Key Point 4: Ways Employees Express Dissatisfaction (EXIT-VOICE-LOYALTY-NEGLECT Model)**

> **1. EXIT (Active/Destructive):**
>    - Leaving the organization
>    - Seeking other jobs
>    - Resigning
>
> **2. VOICE (Active/Constructive):**
>    - Raising concerns to management
>    - Filing complaints or grievances
>    - Suggesting improvements
>    - Joining unions
>
> **3. LOYALTY (Passive/Constructive):**
>    - Waiting passively for conditions to improve
>    - Trusting management will resolve issues
>    - Continuing to support the organization publicly
>
> **4. NEGLECT (Passive/Destructive):**
>    - Reduced effort and quality
>    - Chronic absenteeism
>    - Arriving late, leaving early
>    - Higher error rates and accidents

## ❓ Question 4: Discuss the Big Five Model of personality in detail. Why may personality be useful in managing an organization?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

The **Big Five Model of Personality** (also called the **Five-Factor Model / OCEAN Model**) is the most widely accepted and empirically validated framework for understanding human personality. Developed by **Costa and McCrae (1992)**, it identifies five broad, universal personality dimensions that capture the core of human personality differences.

> 📌 *"The Big Five are broad, bipolar dimensions of personality that emerge consistently across cultures and represent the most comprehensive model of personality."*

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: The Five Dimensions (OCEAN)**

> **1. Openness to Experience (O)**
> - High: Creative, curious, imaginative, broad interests, adventurous
> - Low: Conventional, practical, resistant to change, narrow interests
> - OB Relevance: High openness → better at creative jobs, innovation, training
>
> **2. Conscientiousness (C)**
> - High: Organized, dependable, disciplined, goal-oriented, reliable
> - Low: Impulsive, careless, disorganized, unreliable
> - OB Relevance: **Strongest predictor of job performance** across all jobs
>
> **3. Extraversion (E)**
> - High: Sociable, assertive, talkative, energetic, optimistic
> - Low (Introversion): Reserved, quiet, thoughtful, independent
> - OB Relevance: Predicts leadership, sales success, and team collaboration
>
> **4. Agreeableness (A)**
> - High: Cooperative, trusting, helpful, empathetic, conflict-averse
> - Low: Competitive, suspicious, argumentative, self-interested
> - OB Relevance: High agreeableness → better teamwork, customer service
>
> **5. Neuroticism / Emotional Stability (N)**
> - High Neuroticism: Anxious, moody, insecure, emotionally reactive
> - Low Neuroticism (Stable): Calm, secure, confident, resilient
> - OB Relevance: Low neuroticism → better stress management, leadership

**Key Point 2: Why Personality is Useful in Managing Organizations**

> 1. **Employee Selection:** Big Five scores predict job performance — especially conscientiousness. Used in pre-employment personality tests.
>
> 2. **Team Building:** Agreeableness and extraversion help predict team harmony and collaboration effectiveness.
>
> 3. **Leadership Development:** Extraversion + conscientiousness + openness predict effective leaders.
>
> 4. **Job Placement:** Matching personality to job type (Holland's theory + Big Five) increases satisfaction and reduces turnover.
>
> 5. **Training & Development:** Openness to experience predicts training success and adaptability.
>
> 6. **Conflict Management:** Understanding neuroticism and agreeableness helps predict and prevent interpersonal conflicts.
>
> 7. **Performance Management:** Low conscientious employees may need more supervision and structure.

## ❓ Question 5: What do you mean by job satisfaction? What are the causes of job satisfaction? How is job satisfaction measured? What is job-related attitudes? What is development of personality?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Job Satisfaction** is the degree to which an individual feels positively or negatively about their job. It is a collection of attitudes about various aspects of the job and work environment.

> 📌 *"Job satisfaction is a pleasurable or positive emotional state resulting from the appraisal of one's job or job experiences."* — Edwin Locke

**Job-Related Attitudes** are specific attitudes employees hold toward their job, organization, and work environment. The three most studied are: Job Satisfaction, Job Involvement, and Organizational Commitment.

**Development of Personality** refers to the ongoing process through which an individual's characteristic ways of thinking, feeling, and behaving are formed and refined over time through heredity, environment, and experience.

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Causes / Factors of Job Satisfaction**

> 1. **Work Itself** — Interesting, challenging, and meaningful work leads to satisfaction
> 2. **Pay & Benefits** — Fair and competitive compensation is a basic satisfier
> 3. **Promotion Opportunities** — Career growth prospects motivate employees
> 4. **Supervision Quality** — Supportive, fair, and competent supervisors increase satisfaction
> 5. **Co-worker Relations** — Friendly, cooperative colleagues create a positive work climate
> 6. **Working Conditions** — Safe, comfortable physical environment
> 7. **Recognition & Appreciation** — Being valued for contributions
> 8. **Job Security** — Stability reduces anxiety and increases satisfaction
> 9. **Autonomy** — Freedom to make decisions increases ownership and satisfaction

**Key Point 2: How Job Satisfaction is Measured**

> **1. Single Global Rating Method:**
>    - One overall question: "How satisfied are you with your job overall?" (1–10 scale)
>    - Simple but misses specifics
>
> **2. Summation of Job Facets (Facet Satisfaction):**
>    - Measures satisfaction across multiple dimensions separately
>    - Example: Satisfaction with pay + supervision + colleagues + work itself
>    - Most commonly used: **Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire (MSQ)**
>
> **3. Job Descriptive Index (JDI):**
>    - Measures satisfaction on 5 dimensions: work, pay, promotion, supervision, co-workers
>
> **4. Critical Incidents Method:**
>    - Employees describe events that made them feel exceptionally satisfied or dissatisfied
>    - Used to identify specific satisfiers and dissatisfiers

**Key Point 3: Job-Related Attitudes**

> **1. Job Satisfaction:**
>    Positive/negative feelings toward the job itself
>
> **2. Job Involvement:**
>    The degree to which a person identifies with their job and considers it central to their self-concept. High involvement → extra effort, lower absenteeism.
>
> **3. Organizational Commitment:**
>    - **Affective Commitment** — Emotional attachment ("I love this company")
>    - **Continuance Commitment** — Cost of leaving ("I need this job")
>    - **Normative Commitment** — Moral obligation ("I should stay")
>
> **4. Perceived Organizational Support (POS):**
>    Belief that the organization values your contributions and cares about your wellbeing.

**Key Point 4: Development of Personality**

> Personality develops through:
> - **Stage Theories:** Erikson's 8 Stages of psychosocial development
> - **Early Childhood:** Foundation laid by family environment, parenting style
> - **Adolescence:** Peer influence, identity formation
> - **Adulthood:** Work experiences, relationships, challenges shape further development
> - **Adult Development:** Research shows personality continues to evolve — conscientiousness increases, neuroticism decreases with age
> - **Organizational Impact:** Work environment, mentors, training programs contribute to personality development

## ❓ Question 6: Can attitude be changed in the workplace? If yes, then how? What is attitude? How does attitude affect organizational behaviour?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Attitude** is a learned predisposition to evaluate and respond to a person, object, or situation in a consistently favourable or unfavourable way. Attitudes have three components: cognitive (belief), affective (emotion), and behavioural (action tendency).

**Yes — attitudes CAN be changed**, and this is one of the most important goals of organizational behaviour. Since attitudes influence behaviour, changing attitudes is a key tool for improving organizational performance, culture, and employee relations.

> 📌 *"Attitudes are acquired, and what is learned can be relearned or unlearned."* — Organizational Psychology

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: What is Attitude?**

> - Attitudes are **learned** — not innate
> - They are **relatively stable** but not permanent
> - They have **three components** (ABC: Affective, Behavioural, Cognitive)
> - Every attitude is **object-specific** (directed at a specific thing)
> - Attitudes **predict behaviour** but are not perfectly correlated with it
> - They exist along a **spectrum** from strongly negative to strongly positive

**Key Point 2: How Attitude Affects Organizational Behaviour**

> 1. **Job Performance:**
>    Positive attitudes → Higher effort, creativity, and output quality
>    Negative attitudes → Low motivation, poor quality, resistance
>
> 2. **Absenteeism & Turnover:**
>    Negative job attitudes → More absences, intention to quit
>
> 3. **Teamwork & Cooperation:**
>    Cooperative attitudes → Better team synergy and less conflict
>    Competitive/hostile attitudes → Team dysfunction
>
> 4. **Customer Service:**
>    Positive employee attitudes toward customers → Better service experience
>
> 5. **Organizational Commitment:**
>    Positive attitudes toward the organization → Higher loyalty and OCB
>
> 6. **Resistance to Change:**
>    Negative attitudes toward organizational change → Slowdown in adaptation

**Key Point 3: Can Attitude Be Changed? — YES, HOW?**

> **Methods of Attitude Change in the Workplace:**
>
> **1. Persuasive Communication:**
>    - Management communicating the benefits of new policies
>    - Town halls, internal newsletters, leadership messaging
>    - Credibility and trustworthiness of the source matters
>
> **2. Role Playing:**
>    - Asking employees to play opposite roles (e.g., manager's perspective)
>    - Creates empathy and changes negative attitudes toward management decisions
>
> **3. Training & Education:**
>    - Diversity training, sensitivity training, leadership programs
>    - Providing new information changes cognitive component → attitude shift
>
> **4. Co-worker Influence / Social Pressure:**
>    - Peer groups have powerful influence on individual attitudes
>    - Positive team culture shapes individual member attitudes
>
> **5. Cognitive Dissonance Reduction:**
>    - When attitude and behaviour conflict, one must change
>    - Managers can create situations where employees *behave* positively → attitude follows
>    - Example: Assigning someone who dislikes teamwork to a successful team project
>
> **6. Management by Example (Role Modeling):**
>    - Leaders demonstrating positive attitudes create a cascading effect
>
> **7. Participation & Involvement:**
>    - Involving employees in decision-making changes attitudes toward change
>    - People support what they help create (Participative Management)
>
> **8. Reward & Recognition:**
>    - Reinforcing positive attitudes through rewards makes them persist

#### 💡 Example

> **Cognitive Dissonance Example at Work:**
> An employee who strongly *dislikes* cross-functional teamwork is assigned to a critical project team. Over months, she collaborates, contributes, and the team succeeds. Her behaviour (team player) creates dissonance with her attitude (anti-teamwork). To resolve this dissonance, her attitude shifts: "Actually, teamwork is valuable."
>
> **Diversity Training (Real Case):**
> Companies like **Microsoft** and **Accenture** conduct mandatory unconscious bias training. Studies show this training measurably shifts employee attitudes toward diversity and inclusion — demonstrating attitude change at scale.

#### 🔚 Conclusion

> Attitudes are not fixed — they are dynamic, learnable, and changeable. In the organizational context, managers have numerous tools — from training and communication to participatory management and cognitive dissonance — to shift employee attitudes in a positive direction. Since attitudes are strong predictors of behaviour, investing in attitude change is one of the highest-return investments an organization can make in its human capital. A culture of positive attitudes is the foundation of high performance, low attrition, and sustained organizational success.

## ❓ Question 1: What is organizational effectiveness? Define the term organizational behaviour. How does it impact organizational success?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

**Organizational Effectiveness** refers to the degree to which an organization achieves its goals and objectives efficiently using available resources. It measures how well an organization fulfills its mission and satisfies the needs of its stakeholders.

**Organizational Behaviour (OB)** is the systematic study of human behaviour within organizational settings — examining how individuals, groups, and structures interact and influence one another to improve overall organizational performance.

> 📌 *"OB is the study and application of knowledge about how people, individuals, and groups act in organizations."* — Keith Davis

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: What is Organizational Effectiveness?**

> Organizational effectiveness is measured through multiple criteria:
> - **Goal Attainment** – Degree to which set targets are met
> - **Resource Acquisition** – Ability to secure needed inputs (capital, talent)
> - **Internal Process** – Smooth internal functioning without conflict
> - **Strategic Constituency** – Satisfying all key stakeholders
>
> It reflects the overall health and productivity of an organization in achieving long-term sustainability.

**Key Point 2: Definition & Nature of Organizational Behaviour**

> OB is an **interdisciplinary field** drawing from:
> - **Psychology** – Motivation, personality, perception
> - **Sociology** – Group dynamics, team behaviour
> - **Anthropology** – Culture, values, customs
> - **Political Science** – Power, conflict, leadership
>
> Key characteristics of OB:
> - It is **goal-oriented** (aims to improve performance)
> - It is **humanistic** (values human dignity)
> - It follows a **systems approach** (views organisation as a whole)
> - It is **situational** (behaviour depends on context)

**Key Point 3: Impact of OB on Organizational Success**

> OB directly impacts success through:
> - **Improved Employee Motivation** → Higher productivity
> - **Better Leadership** → Effective decision-making
> - **Team Cohesion** → Reduced conflicts, better collaboration
> - **Change Management** → Employees adapt faster to change
> - **Job Satisfaction** → Lower attrition rates
> - **Organizational Culture** → Positive environment attracts talent

## ❓ Question 2: Why is the subject organizational behaviour incorporated in the syllabus of the technology and management courses?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

Organizational Behaviour (OB) is the study of how people behave in organizational settings, both individually and in groups. It has been incorporated into **technology (B.Tech)** and **management (MBA/BBA)** curricula because modern engineers and managers must not only possess technical expertise but also possess the **human and behavioural skills** required to lead teams, manage change, and build effective organizations.

> 📌 *"Technical knowledge takes you to the door — behavioural knowledge opens it."*

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Bridging Technical Skills with Human Skills**

> Engineers and technologists often work in **cross-functional teams**. Without understanding human behaviour, even the most technically brilliant professional may fail as a team leader or project manager.
>
> OB teaches:
> - How to **motivate** team members
> - How to **resolve conflicts** in project teams
> - How to communicate technical ideas to non-technical stakeholders
>
> This is why B.Tech programmes include OB — to create **complete professionals**, not just technical experts.

**Key Point 2: Prepares Students for Real-World Organizations**

> Today's workplace is **dynamic and diverse** — multicultural teams, remote work, AI integration, and rapid change are common. OB prepares students to:
> - **Adapt to change** using change management theories
> - **Work in diverse teams** with cultural sensitivity
> - **Exercise leadership** using different leadership styles (transformational, transactional)
> - **Manage stress** and maintain work-life balance
>
> These are critical soft skills that technical education alone cannot provide.

**Key Point 3: Foundation for Management Roles**

> Many engineering graduates eventually move into **managerial roles** (Project Manager, Product Manager, CTO). OB provides the theoretical and practical foundation for:
> - **Decision-making** under uncertainty
> - **Performance appraisal** of subordinates
> - **Organizational design** and restructuring
> - **Employee development** and HRM practices
>
> Without OB knowledge, managers risk poor people management, leading to high attrition and low productivity.

## ❓ Question 3: What is the historical background of organizational behaviour? What are dependent variables in the organizational behaviour model?

### ✅ Answer:

#### 📖 Definition / Introduction

The history of Organizational Behaviour traces the evolution of management thought — from purely mechanical and task-focused approaches to a holistic understanding of human behaviour in the workplace. OB as a formal discipline emerged in the **20th century**, though its roots go back to earlier management theories.

**Dependent Variables** in the OB model are the key outcomes that researchers and managers aim to **explain and predict** — they are the "effects" that result from various human and organizational factors.

---

#### 📝 Detailed Explanation

**Key Point 1: Historical Background of OB**

> **1. Classical Era (1900–1930) — Scientific Management**
> - **Frederick W. Taylor** introduced *Scientific Management* (1911)
> - Focus: Maximizing efficiency through time-and-motion studies
> - Workers were treated as "economic machines"
> - **Henri Fayol** defined 14 principles of management (planning, organizing, controlling)
> - **Max Weber** introduced the concept of *Bureaucracy* — hierarchical structure with rules
>
> **2. Human Relations Movement (1930–1950)**
> - **Elton Mayo** conducted the famous **Hawthorne Studies** (1924–1932) at Western Electric Company
> - Discovery: Worker productivity is influenced by **social factors** and **attention given to workers** — not just physical conditions
> - This was a **turning point** — human behaviour was recognized as critical
> - Led to focus on morale, group dynamics, and informal organizations
>
> **3. Behavioural Science Era (1950–1970)**
> - Scholars like **Abraham Maslow** (Hierarchy of Needs), **Douglas McGregor** (Theory X & Y), and **Frederick Herzberg** (Two-Factor Theory) contributed
> - Focus shifted to motivation, personality, and individual needs
> - OB became more scientific and research-based
>
> **4. Modern OB Era (1970–Present)**
> - Integration of psychology, sociology, and anthropology
> - Emergence of concepts: Emotional Intelligence, Organizational Culture, Diversity Management, Remote Work behaviour
> - Use of **data analytics** and **AI** to study and predict behaviour

**Key Point 2: OB Model — Dependent Variables**

> Dependent variables are the **outcomes** that OB seeks to understand, predict, and improve:
>
> 1. **Productivity** — Efficiency (output per unit input) + Effectiveness (goal achievement). OB helps improve both.
>
> 2. **Absenteeism** — Frequency of employees missing work. High absenteeism indicates low morale and poor job satisfaction. OB helps identify and address root causes.
>
> 3. **Turnover** — Rate at which employees leave the organisation. High turnover is costly and disrupts operations. OB helps retain talent through motivation and engagement.
>
> 4. **Organizational Citizenship Behaviour (OCB)** — Voluntary actions beyond job description (helping colleagues, staying late). OB encourages a culture where OCB thrives.
>
> 5. **Job Satisfaction** — An individual's emotional state about their job. Satisfied employees are more productive, creative, and loyal. OB identifies factors that drive satisfaction.
>
> 6. **Deviant Workplace Behaviour** — Voluntary actions that violate norms and harm the organization (theft, harassment, sabotage). OB helps detect and prevent such behaviour.

**Key Point 3: Hawthorne Effect — The Foundation of Modern OB**

> The **Hawthorne Studies** revealed that workers perform better when they know they are being observed and when they feel valued. This established that **human behaviour** — not just physical conditions — drives productivity. This insight laid the foundation for modern OB, shifting focus from machines to **people**.

#### 💡 Example

> **Hawthorne Effect (Real Example):** During the Hawthorne Studies at Western Electric's Chicago plant, Mayo's team found that productivity improved when workers were observed — regardless of lighting or break conditions. The key factor was **human attention and recognition**.
>
> **Modern Example:** During **COVID-19**, companies studied OB to understand how remote work affected productivity (dependent variable), job satisfaction, and absenteeism. Companies like **Microsoft** and **Infosys** redesigned their people policies based on OB research to maintain high performance.

#### 🔚 Conclusion

> The historical evolution of OB — from Taylor's Scientific Management to Mayo's Human Relations and modern behavioural science — reflects a growing recognition that **people are the most valuable asset** of any organization. The dependent variables (productivity, satisfaction, turnover, absenteeism, OCB, deviance) serve as measurable benchmarks for organizational health. Understanding these variables allows managers to design interventions that lead to sustainable organizational success.
